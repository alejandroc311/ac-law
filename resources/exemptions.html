Bankruptcy Exemptions: What You Keep When You File

Initial Case Review Or call (904) 555-0123

One of the biggest concerns people have when considering bankruptcy is whether they will lose everything they own. That fear is understandable, but in most cases it is misplaced. Bankruptcy law is designed to give people a financial reset, not to strip them of basic necessities or long term security. Exemptions are the legal mechanism that makes that possible.

An exemption is a law that protects certain types of property from being taken by creditors or the bankruptcy trustee. In practical terms, exemptions determine what property you keep when you file. In many consumer cases, exemptions protect all or nearly all of a person’s assets, which is why most bankruptcy filings result in no property being liquidated.

Federal, Florida, and Puerto Rico Exemptions

Exemptions come from both federal and local law. The federal exemption system is set forth in 11 U.S. Code § 522. In addition to the federal exemptions, each state and territory has its own exemption laws that apply to residents who file bankruptcy there.

Florida has opted out of the federal exemption system. That means Florida residents are required to use Florida exemptions rather than the federal ones. Florida exemptions are found primarily in Chapter 222 of the Florida Statutes, along with additional protections located in other statutes and the Florida Constitution.

Puerto Rico operates differently. In Puerto Rico, debtors may generally choose between the federal exemptions and the Puerto Rico exemption system found in 32 L.P.R.A. § 1130. In practice, many Puerto Rico residents rely on the local exemptions when the primary goal is protecting a home or a vehicle. Puerto Rico’s exemption statutes are relatively narrow and have not been substantially updated in many years, so outside of homestead and basic transportation, the federal exemptions are often more favorable for protecting personal property and financial assets.

Which exemption system applies, and which one makes the most sense, depends on residency rules and the specific assets involved.

Retirement Accounts and Pension Benefits

Retirement savings are among the most strongly protected assets in bankruptcy. In most cases, tax qualified retirement accounts are exempt from creditors and from the bankruptcy estate. The policy behind this protection is straightforward. Bankruptcy is not meant to eliminate a person’s ability to retire or support themselves in the future.

For most filers, retirement accounts remain untouched, even in Chapter 7 cases.

Social Security, Disability, and Public Benefits

Income derived from public benefits is generally protected in bankruptcy. This includes Social Security benefits, disability benefits, veterans benefits, and similar forms of assistance. These funds are intended to cover basic living expenses and are usually exempt even after being deposited into a bank account, as long as they can be properly identified.

The law recognizes that taking these benefits would undermine their purpose.

Life Insurance and Annuity Proceeds

Many exemption laws protect the proceeds and value of life insurance policies and certain annuities. These protections exist because insurance is often tied to family support and long term planning. Depending on the applicable exemption scheme, both death benefits and the cash value of certain policies may be shielded from creditors.

Homestead and Home Equity

Protection for a primary residence is one of the most significant aspects of bankruptcy exemptions. Federal law provides a homestead exemption with a defined cap. Florida is widely known for its strong homestead protections, which are rooted in the Florida Constitution and can shield substantial home equity when the property qualifies.

Puerto Rico provides particularly strong protection for a primary residence through the hogar seguro. Under Puerto Rico law, a qualifying home may be protected in its entirety, regardless of the amount of equity. For many Puerto Rico residents, this means that their primary residence cannot be taken or sold in a bankruptcy case.

Whether a home is protected depends on compliance with local requirements, but in many cases, the homestead exemption is the cornerstone of asset protection in bankruptcy.

Vehicles and Transportation

Most exemption systems allow a debtor to protect at least one personal vehicle up to a certain value. Transportation is treated as a necessity rather than a luxury. In both Florida and Puerto Rico, vehicle exemptions are often a key consideration, particularly for people who rely on their car to work and support their family.

Personal Property and Everyday Belongings

Bankruptcy is not designed to take ordinary household items. Clothing, furniture, household goods, and personal effects are generally protected. These items typically have little resale value and are essential to daily living, which is why exemption laws consistently protect them.

Why Exemptions Matter

Exemptions are technical and fact dependent. How property is titled, how long it has been owned, how funds were handled, and which exemption system applies can all affect the outcome of a case. Two people with similar assets can have very different results depending on jurisdiction and planning.

That is why exemptions are one of the most important parts of a bankruptcy case. When applied correctly, they protect what matters most and allow the process to work as intended.
